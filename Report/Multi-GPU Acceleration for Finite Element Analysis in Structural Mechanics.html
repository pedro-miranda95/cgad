Multi-GPU Acceleration for Finite Element Analysis in Structural Mechanics

1. Introduction

The increasing complexity of engineering systems and the demand for accurate numerical predictions have made computational simulation an indispensable tool in modern engineering and applied sciences. Among numerical techniques, the Finite Element Method (FEM) stands out as one of the most robust and versatile approaches for solving boundary-value problems governed by partial differential equations (PDEs). FEM is particularly dominant in structural mechanics, where it enables the analysis of displacements, strains, and stresses in complex geometries under realistic loading and boundary conditions.

Despite its conceptual elegance and widespread adoption, FEM is computationally expensive. As model fidelity increases—through mesh refinement, higher-dimensional domains, or parametric studies—the size of the resulting algebraic systems grows rapidly. In practical applications, these systems often involve millions of degrees of freedom, leading to significant computational costs and memory requirements. The main performance bottlenecks arise from the assembly of large sparse matrices and, most notably, from the iterative solution of sparse linear systems.

In recent years, Graphics Processing Units (GPUs) have emerged as powerful accelerators for scientific computing. Originally designed for graphics rendering, GPUs now provide massive data-parallel execution capabilities and high memory bandwidth, making them particularly suitable for linear algebra operations that dominate FEM workloads. At the same time, modern software ecosystems—especially Python-based frameworks such as CuPy, Numba, and Dask-CUDA—have significantly lowered the barrier to GPU programming, enabling rapid prototyping while retaining high performance.

Within this context, this project proposes the development of a GPU-accelerated Finite Element Analysis solver for structural mechanics, progressing from a CPU baseline to single-GPU and multi-GPU execution. The project aims not only to achieve performance improvements but also to provide a rigorous analysis of parallelisation strategies, GPU execution models, and scalability, in line with the objectives of the High Performance Graphical Computing curricular unit.

2. The Finite Element Method (FEM)

2.1 Conceptual Foundations

The Finite Element Method is a numerical discretisation technique for approximating the solution of PDEs over complex domains. The fundamental idea is to decompose a continuous physical domain into a finite set of smaller subdomains, known as elements, which are interconnected at nodes. Within each element, the unknown field variables (e.g., displacement, temperature, or electric potential) are approximated using polynomial shape functions.

2.2 Importance and Role of FEM

FEM plays a central role in engineering analysis and design because it allows engineers and scientists to:

  • model complex geometries that are intractable analytically,

  • incorporate heterogeneous and anisotropic material properties,

  • apply realistic boundary and loading conditions,

  • perform virtual prototyping and reduce reliance on physical experiments.

In structural mechanics, FEM is extensively used in civil, mechanical, aerospace, and automotive engineering for tasks ranging from static stress analysis to vibration, fatigue, and failure assessment. Its predictive power and flexibility have made FEM a standard component of industrial design workflows and regulatory compliance processes.

3. Types of Problems Addressed by FEM

The Finite Element Method is applicable to a wide spectrum of physical problems, including:

3.1 Linear Static Structural Problems

These involve the computation of displacements, strains, and stresses under static loading conditions. Linear elastic assumptions lead to symmetric positive definite systems, making them well-suited for iterative solvers and performance benchmarking.

3.2 Dynamic and Modal Analysis

FEM can be extended to time-dependent problems, enabling the study of transient responses, natural frequencies, and vibration modes of structures.

3.3 Thermo-Mechanical and Multi-Physics Problems

Coupled phenomena, such as heat transfer combined with structural deformation, can be modelled using FEM, although at increased computational cost.

3.4 Nonlinear Problems

Advanced FEM formulations handle geometric nonlinearities, material plasticity, and contact mechanics. While highly relevant in practice, such problems are computationally demanding and complex.

In this project, the focus is deliberately placed on linear elastic structural problems, which provide a mathematically well-posed and computationally controlled environment for evaluating GPU acceleration strategies while remaining representative of real-world engineering applications.

4. Computational Motivation for GPU and Multi-GPU Acceleration

From a computational standpoint, FEM workloads are dominated by linear algebra operations, particularly sparse matrix–vector multiplications (SpMV) and vector updates performed repeatedly within iterative solvers such as the Conjugate Gradient (CG) method. These operations exhibit a high degree of data parallelism, as the same arithmetic operations are applied independently to large sets of data.

GPUs follow the SIMT (Single Instruction, Multiple Threads) execution model, which allows thousands of lightweight threads to execute the same instruction on different data elements simultaneously. This makes GPUs especially effective for accelerating the core kernels of FEM solvers.

However, single-GPU acceleration alone may be insufficient for large-scale studies or parametric analyses. Multi-GPU execution provides an additional level of scalability, enabling either the distribution of independent simulation cases across GPUs or the partitioning of computational workloads. Frameworks such as Dask-CUDA facilitate this process by abstracting device management and task scheduling.

5. Project Objectives

5.1 General Objective

The primary objective of this project is to design, implement, and evaluate a GPU-accelerated Finite Element Analysis solver for structural mechanics, assessing performance gains and scalability relative to a CPU-based baseline.

5.2 Specific Objectives

The specific objectives are:

  • CPU Baseline Development
    Implement a complete and numerically correct FEM solver on the CPU using Python, NumPy, and SciPy, serving as a reference for correctness and performance.

  • Bottleneck Identification
    Analyse the FEM pipeline to identify computationally dominant components, particularly sparse linear system solvers.

  • Single-GPU Acceleration
    Accelerate performance-critical components using CuPy and GPU-enabled sparse linear algebra routines.

  • Multi-GPU Execution
    Extend the solver to multi-GPU execution using Dask-CUDA, enabling parallel execution of multiple FEM simulations.

  • Performance Benchmarking and Analysis
    Quantitatively compare CPU, single-GPU, and multi-GPU implementations in terms of execution time, speedup, and scalability.

  • Demonstration and Reproducibility
    Develop a demonstrable and reproducible application that presents both physical simulation results and performance metrics in an interactive environment.

7. Case Study: Potential Flow in a Channel with Geometric Constrictions

7.1 Physical Problem Description

The specific case study analysed in this work consists of a two-dimensional potential flow of an incompressible and inviscid fluid inside a channel with geometric constrictions formed by circular obstacles. The channel is assumed to be infinite in the out-of-plane direction, allowing for a 2D formulation.

A uniform inflow velocity is imposed at the inlet, and the objective is to determine:

  • the velocity potential distribution,
  • the velocity field,
  • the pressure distribution,
  • the maximum and minimum values of velocity and pressure and their locations.

This configuration is representative of internal flows in ducts and channels and provides a demanding benchmark due to the presence of strong velocity gradients near the constrictions.

The flow is described using the velocity potential ϕ(x,y)\phi(x,y)ϕ(x,y), such that:

7.3 Boundary Conditions

The problem involves mixed boundary conditions, defined as follows:

Inlet Boundary (Neumann)

At the inlet section of the channel, a uniform normal velocity is imposed:

∂ϕ∂n=V0=2.5  m/s\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial n} = V_0 = 2.5 \; \text{m/s}∂n∂ϕ​=V0​=2.5m/s

This corresponds to a natural (Neumann) boundary condition.

Outlet Boundary (Dirichlet)

At the outlet sections, the velocity potential is set to zero:

ϕ=0\phi = 0ϕ=0

This essential (Dirichlet) boundary condition defines a reference level for the potential.

Wall Boundaries

Along all solid boundaries of the domain, including the circular constrictions, a no-penetration condition is enforced. This condition states that the normal component of the velocity vanishes at the walls, which in terms of the velocity potential can be written as:

∂ϕ∂n=0\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial n} = 0∂n∂ϕ​=0

This boundary condition guarantees that the fluid remains confined within the channel and that no mass flux occurs across the solid surfaces.

7.4 Mesh Refinement Strategy

To accurately capture high-gradient regions near the geometric constrictions, a locally refined mesh is employed in the central region of the channel, while coarser elements are used elsewhere.

Mesh refinement leads to a significant increase in the number of degrees of freedom, making this case particularly suitable for evaluating the performance and scalability of GPU and multi-GPU FEM implementations.
